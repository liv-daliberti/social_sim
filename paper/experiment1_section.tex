\section{Setting and Markets}
\label{sec:setting}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

The central diagnostic question of our first experiment is whether frontier language agents build and
maintain coherent event models that support consistent Bayesian updating, or whether their forecasts
are better described as evidence-insensitive outputs that happen to match empirical base rates.  We
address this in a prospective setting: all markets we study were \emph{unresolved} at the time of
agent interaction, precluding direct memorization of outcomes.

We select binary Polymarket markets satisfying the following criteria: open at the time of sampling
(June~2026), resolving between 14 and 180~days from the sampling date, Polymarket CLOB volume
$\geq 1{,}000$ transactions, and belonging to one of five thematic categories---Politics, Government,
Elections, Economics, or Geopolitics.  This yields a candidate pool of 83~markets.  From these we
draw a diverse pilot set of five markets spanning two domains (Middle East geopolitics, U.S.\
elections) and a range of market-implied probabilities (8--41\%).  Table~\ref{tab:markets} lists the
five markets together with their Polymarket CLOB mid-prices at the time of agent elicitation.

\section{Structured Forecast Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

For each market we run two frontier agents---GPT-5.4 and Claude Opus~4.8, both deployed via Microsoft
Azure AI Foundry with live web-search access---through a four-stage multi-turn protocol illustrated in
Figure~\ref{fig:exp1-results}(A).

\paragraph{Stage 1 --- Event model and initial forecast.}
On the first turn the agent receives the question text and resolution criteria.  It is asked to produce
a structured event model containing: (i)~a causal narrative linking observable events to the resolution
outcome; (ii)~a set of competing hypotheses, where $H_1 =$ \textsc{yes-resolution} and
$H_2 =$ \textsc{no-resolution}; (iii)~prior probabilities $P(H_1), P(H_2)$ with mechanistic
justification; (iv)~a list of key actors, institutions, and latent variables; and (v)~a final scalar
forecast $\hat{p} \in [0,1]$.  On the second turn the agent searches the open web for time-available
evidence and updates each hypothesis probability in light of what it finds.  An optional third turn
deepens research in areas identified as under-evidenced.  A final turn elicits a structured JSON
forecast.  Each market-agent combination is run $k = 5$ times with independent random seeds; the
per-market point estimate is the median across runs.

\paragraph{Stage 2 --- Counterfactual evidence packets.}
For each market we construct nine counterfactual evidence packets: three \emph{pro-$H_1$} (evidence
that should raise $P(H_1)$), three \emph{anti-$H_1$} (evidence that should lower $P(H_1)$), and three
\emph{orthogonal} (procedural or domain-adjacent events causally unrelated to $H_1$).  Each packet
contains a synthetic news headline, a one-sentence mechanism description, and a slot-type label from a
taxonomy of evidence classes (data releases, diplomatic signals, logistical events, procedural
announcements; see Appendix~\ref{app:cf-taxonomy}).  Packets are designed so that the direction of
implication is unambiguous to any reader who accepts the headline as true.

As an illustration, for the question \textit{``Strait of Hormuz traffic returns to normal by end of
June?''} the pro-$H_1$ packet reads \textit{``Oman says Iran agrees daily protected windows for
commercial Hormuz transits''} (mechanism: bilateral arrangement enables regular passage); the matched
orthogonal packet reads \textit{``IMO opens technical comment round on Gulf vessel incident reporting
format''} (administrative rulemaking with no causal link to transit volumes).  Table~\ref{tab:cf-example}
in Appendix~\ref{app:cf-taxonomy} gives full examples for two markets.

\paragraph{Stage 3 --- Counterfactual updates.}
Each counterfactual packet is presented to each of the $k = 5$ initial runs, yielding
$9 \times 5 = 45$ update records per market per model.  The agent receives its own Stage~1
structured forecast together with a single new evidence headline and is asked to revise both
hypothesis posteriors and the final forecast accordingly.  Web search is not available at this
stage; the update is driven purely by the injected evidence.  Across five markets this produces
225~update records for Claude Opus~4.8 and 90~update records for GPT-5.4 (see
Appendix~\ref{app:data} for details on run counts).

\section{Consistency Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

For each update record $i$ we compute three binary consistency indicators.  Let $d_i \in
\{+1,-1\}$ denote the intended direction of the counterfactual ($+1$ for pro-$H_1$, $-1$ for
anti-$H_1$); orthogonal updates are excluded from direction-sensitive metrics.

\paragraph{Evidence--Hypothesis Consistency (EHC).}
EHC tests whether the agent's \emph{hypothesis-level} belief moves in the intended direction.
Let $\Delta p(H_1)_i = p(H_1)_i^{\text{post}} - p(H_1)_i^{\text{prior}}$.  We count only updates
where $|\Delta p(H_1)_i| \geq 0.03$ (a meaningful update occurred):
\begin{equation}
    \text{EHC}_i = \mathbf{1}\!\left[\Delta p(H_1)_i \cdot d_i > 0\right],
    \qquad
    \overline{\text{EHC}} = \frac{1}{N_\text{EHC}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_\text{EHC}} \text{EHC}_i.
    \label{eq:ehc}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Hypothesis--Forecast Consistency (HFC).}
HFC tests whether the final output $\hat{p}$ moves in the same direction.  Let
$\Delta\hat{p}_i = \hat{p}_i^{\text{post}} - \hat{p}_i^{\text{prior}}$, counted when
$|\Delta\hat{p}_i| \geq 0.03$:
\begin{equation}
    \text{HFC}_i = \mathbf{1}\!\left[\Delta\hat{p}_i \cdot d_i > 0\right],
    \qquad
    \overline{\text{HFC}} = \frac{1}{N_\text{HFC}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_\text{HFC}} \text{HFC}_i.
    \label{eq:hfc}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Internal Coherence Score (ICS).}
For this two-hypothesis model the structural invariant is $\hat{p} = P(H_1)$.  ICS fires when
the updated values satisfy this invariant within 2~percentage points:
\begin{equation}
    \text{ICS}_i = \mathbf{1}\!\left[\bigl|\hat{p}_i^{\text{post}} - p(H_1)_i^{\text{post}}\bigr| < 0.02\right],
    \qquad
    \overline{\text{ICS}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \text{ICS}_i.
    \label{eq:ics}
\end{equation}
ICS is evaluated on \emph{all} update records (including orthogonal) and does not require a
threshold on update magnitude.

Together these three metrics decompose the consistency question: EHC tests evidence-to-hypothesis
propagation; HFC tests hypothesis-to-forecast propagation; ICS tests internal structural validity.
High EHC with low HFC is the signature of an agent that ``understands'' the evidence at the
hypothesis level but anchors its headline number to the prior.  High EHC and HFC with low ICS
indicates directionally correct but structurally incoherent updates.

\paragraph{Anchoring baseline.}
A complementary baseline tests whether directional evidence moves the forecast \emph{more} than
causally irrelevant evidence.  We define the \emph{sensitivity ratio}
\begin{equation}
    \text{Sensitivity} = \frac{\overline{|\Delta\hat{p}|}_{\text{pro}+\text{anti}}}
                              {\overline{|\Delta\hat{p}|}_{\text{orthogonal}}},
    \label{eq:sensitivity}
\end{equation}
where the numerator pools pro- and anti-$H_1$ updates and the denominator uses orthogonal updates.
Sensitivity $\approx 1$ indicates that the model revises equally regardless of causal
relevance---a failure of selectivity we term \emph{evidence-insensitive anchoring}.
Sensitivity $\gg 1$ indicates that the model reserves substantive revision for directionally
relevant evidence.

\section{Experiment 1: Prospective Forecasting Results}
\label{sec:exp1-results}
% ------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/exp1_results.pdf}
\caption{
\textbf{Prospective forecasting results.}
\emph{(A)} Four-stage pipeline: structured initial forecast ($k=5$ runs per market), counterfactual
packet construction (9 per market), evidence injection and update (45 records per model), and
consistency evaluation.
\emph{(B)} Mean absolute forecast revision $\overline{|\Delta\hat{p}|}$ by counterfactual direction
for each agent ($\pm 1$~SE).  Both agents revise substantially for directional evidence and
negligibly for orthogonal evidence, yielding sensitivity ratios of $34.6\times$ (Claude Opus~4.8)
and $8.5\times$ (GPT-5.4).
\emph{(C)} Agent median forecast vs.\ Polymarket mid-price across all five markets.  Both agents
consistently underestimate market prices ($\overline{|A{-}M|}= 17.7$~pp and $16.9$~pp),
suggesting systematic skepticism relative to crowd belief.}
\label{fig:exp1-results}
\end{figure}

Table~\ref{tab:consistency} reports consistency metrics aggregated across all five markets.
Both agents achieve $\overline{\text{EHC}} = \overline{\text{HFC}} = \overline{\text{ICS}} = 1.00$
(SE~$= 0$, given unanimous passing across all evaluated runs).  Every update in which a
threshold-crossing change occurred moved in the predicted direction, and the two output
fields---hypothesis posterior and headline forecast---remained mutually consistent throughout.

Figure~\ref{fig:exp1-results}(B) displays the anchoring baseline.  Both agents show strongly
direction-selective updating.  For Claude Opus~4.8, pro-$H_1$ evidence produces mean absolute
revisions of $11.3$~pp ($n = 75$), anti-$H_1$ evidence $4.8$~pp ($n = 75$), and orthogonal
evidence only $0.23$~pp ($n = 75$)---a sensitivity ratio of $34.6\times$.  GPT-5.4 shows a
similar pattern: pro-$H_1$ at $8.4$~pp, anti-$H_1$ at $3.6$~pp, and orthogonal at $0.71$~pp
(sensitivity $8.5\times$).  The asymmetry between pro- and anti-$H_1$ magnitudes is consistent
with bounded probability arithmetic near the lower end of the prior distribution, where upward
revisions from low priors can travel further in absolute terms.

Figure~\ref{fig:exp1-results}(C) shows agent forecasts plotted against Polymarket mid-prices.
Both agents are systematically below market prices on all five questions, with mean absolute
divergences of $17.7$~pp (Claude) and $16.9$~pp (GPT).  The gap is largest for the permanent
peace deal market (market price 41\%; both agents forecast $\approx 6$\%), where agents apply
strong base-rate skepticism that is not fully corrected by available web evidence.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The results are consistent with both agents constructing and maintaining coherent, updateable event
models: hypothesis posteriors and headline forecasts co-update in the intended direction for every
directional intervention, while orthogonal interventions produce negligible revisions.  This pattern
is difficult to reconcile with pure retrieval, market imitation, or post-hoc rationalization, each
of which would predict indiscriminate or direction-insensitive updating.  At the same time, the
systematic under-pricing relative to Polymarket suggests that coherence and calibration can come
apart: the agents' world models may be structurally valid yet poorly anchored to the crowd's
aggregated beliefs.

We note three limitations this pilot cannot resolve.  First, the sample of five markets is small;
ceiling effects on EHC, HFC, and ICS preclude ranking the two models.  Second, counterfactual
packets were designed to be unambiguous; adversarial or ambiguous evidence may reveal breakdowns
not visible here.  Third, the absence of web search at Stage~3 prevents agents from cross-checking
injected headlines, which may inflate apparent coherence.  These are addressed in
Appendix~\ref{app:exp1-limitations} and constitute priorities for the full-scale experiment.
